More generally, I've been pondering the quantification of humanity's progress and societal antifragility. In that vein, I've been digging into the following:

  • 1. Democratizing Access: The internet has been absolutely incredible in increasing access to information. Believe it or not, there was once a time where search engines, forums, and thousands of experts sharing their thoughts and opinions on blogs weren't at our disposal when looking to find something that we want to learn more about. Democratizing access is perhaps a phrase that has become far too ubiquitous in today's tech and innovation ecosystem, but I firmly believe that supporting institutions and brands that 1. make things that previously required payment free, 2. make things that used to be costly cheap, or 3. make things that previously had high friction to adopt much more convenient to use are those that will endure and be remembered as uplifting society and contributing to our continued progress.
  • 2. Learning and Preserving Knowledge: The sharing of knowledge between peers, and more challengingly between generations is slowly being facilitated to a greater degree by technological innovation. However, we as a society still struggle to separate signal from noise and keep important insights immortalized in some way, shape, or form. New productivity tools such as Roam Research, Notion, and Mem Labs are contributing to knowledge organization/neural mapping, but I'm still on the lookout for builders working on something in the information permanence space. Educational institutions as a whole are also quickly becoming obsolete, with the alternative learning models offered by Lambda/Make School, Minerva, and Sora Schools becoming more and more enticing by the day by offering greater malleability for learner interests and creativity while still delivering the most essential knowledge for success in today's society.
  • 3. Skin in the Game: Of Nassim Nicholas Taleb's theses, this is the one I subscribe to most strongly. Essentially, Taleb posits that having measurable risk when taking a major decision is necessary for fairness, efficiency, and risk management. Actors must bear some of the cost when they fail. An example that immediately comes to mind is the 2008 financial crisis, where several actors paid no financial penalty when their institutions had to be rescued by U.S. taxpayers due to overreach. This is a sort of "trade" with upside gain but no or limited downside risk. Another example would be a political war hawk that bears no risk whatsoever of dying in a war they agitate for. Societal progress perhaps offers the most sound argument for SITG -- those who err and have SITG are forced to rectify their actions and "right the ship" so to speak, or alternatively, be replaced. Eventually, this should eliminate actors taking part in needlessly reckless actions and contribute overall to more responsible governance, commerce, and institutions.
